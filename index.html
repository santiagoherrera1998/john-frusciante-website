John Frusciante Picks His Bandcamp Favorites

John Frusciante picked his favorite Bandcamp releases and shared them with us all!

Unless you’ve been living under a rock for the past 20 years, you’re undoubtedly aware of John Frusciante’s guitar chops. His soaring riffs and triumphant solos were pivotal in elevating the Red Hot Chili Peppers to international stardom and creative triumph, acting as the cerebral complement to Flea’s funky bass and Anthony Kiedis’ nonsensical raps. There’s another, woefully-unexplored side to Frusciante aside from his work with the Peppers and as a solo artist, though—for the past 13 years, he’s been moonlighting as the electronic auteur Trickfinger, assembling rave music, ambient, acid, UK garage and more into warped, homespun soundscapes. His third album under that alias, She Smiles Because She Presses The Button, arrived last month on the Acid Test sub-label Avenue 66. It marks the culmination of his journey thus far, his meticulous attention to detail and melody reinterpreted from a digitized standpoint.

Frusciante’s introduction to electronic music predates his career arc as a rocker; he first experienced it at age 6, when he heard Donna Summer’s “I Feel Love” on the radio. “I was very into New Wave starting when I was 9, and Eno and stuff, but Rave music really started to take off in ’92 when I was beginning a five-year drug run,” Frusciante says. “I had cut myself off from the world and wasn’t paying attention. When I got clean in 1997, I suddenly felt very drawn to anything with synths, drum machines, and breaks, and I started buying as many different kinds of recent electronic music as I could. Some standout discoveries over that next few years were Aphex Twin (Classics), 808 State (Newbuild), Komakino (Energy Trancemission), The Railway Raver (Drop Acid Not Bombs), Autechre (Tri Repetae), The Prodigy (Experience), Squarepusher (Feed Me Weird Things), and C.J. Bolland (The 4th Sign).” Though he’s technically been making electronic music since 1998, it took nearly another ten years for his endeavors to bear fruit. “It wasn’t until 2007, when the first Trickfinger stuff was made, that I found the right gear for me, and was dead set on the path of being my own engineer.”

Q&A with Ryan Hewitt about TWTD

Q&A with Ryan Hewitt about TWTD

Ryan Hewitt (producer, mixer, engineer) recently did an Ask Me Anything on his Instagram after a fan reached out to him with questions about John Frusciante’s The Will To Death. Ryan received a lot of questions about the recording process of the album. Below is an excerpt, find a full transcript here!

Q: What did you use for the effects in A Doubt? Particularly the higher tone noise in the intro and the whooshing noises at around 2:00. Similarly for the whooshing noises in The Mirror. Synth? Or cymbals with heavy effects? A: Mostly John using his modular synth. It was a huge presence on his records at the time. Unlimited possibilities using that as a processor. He got super creative… Q: What was the odd piano thingy breakdown in Wishing? A: A bunch of music boxes. No I have to correct myself! I think it actually may have been a bunch of piano loops John and Josh made with guitar pedals. Q: What are the main musical and non-musical life lessons you learned from working with John? A: I could write a book!! That man is on another planet from us mere mortals… truly. I just tried to keep up most of the time.

Enclosure (2014)

“Enclosure, upon its completion, was the record which represented the achievement of all the musical goals I had been aiming at for the previous 5 years. It was recorded simultaneously with Black Knights’ Medieval Chamber, and as different as the two albums appear to be, they represent one investigative creative thought process. What I learned from one fed directly into the other. Enclosure is presently my last word on the musical statement which began with PBX".

- John Frusciante
